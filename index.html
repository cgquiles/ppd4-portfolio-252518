Introduction

I am Carlos Granados Quiles, a Logistics Engineering student on the MBO route at Breda University of Applied Sciences. I joined the programme in Year 2 in September 2025, entering directly without completing Year 1 at BUas. That meant I had to build a working understanding of the foundational curriculum while keeping pace with Year 2 coursework at the same time. PLO 1

Research

You research supply chain challenges in a broader context, taking sustainability, internationalisation, and technology into consideration.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence
CIR3B — Waste Disposal Time Study, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
Field research study on non-value-adding waste disposal time in picking operations. Methods: structured time observations (30+), regression analysis, DESTEP. Scope: cable reel SKUs, aisles 316–323.

What I Did

For CIR3B I independently designed and executed a time study at CEVA Logistics in Tilburg. The research question was straightforward: how much non-value-adding time do picking workers spend on waste disposal, and what is driving it?

I started with a DESTEP analysis to frame the broader context. The sustainability angle was immediately relevant — CEVA has a stated target of 95% waste recycling, yet the current container layout forces unnecessary travel for picking workers. The technology angle came in through my use of regression analysis to model the relationship between walking distance and disposal time, rather than relying on simple averages. The internationalisation dimension was present in the regulatory background: EU waste handling requirements and CEVA’s global sustainability commitments both shape how the warehouse is expected to operate.

I carried out over 30 timed field observations across aisles 316 to 323, documenting disposal trips made by picking workers. Early in the process I identified that the original scope — covering replenishment workers — was misaligned with operational reality. Replenishment behaviour was too variable to measure reliably. I recommended a scope change to focus on picking workers. That decision was mine alone. I presented the reasoning to my supervisor, got sign-off, and documented the change explicitly. I then narrowed the study to cable reel SKUs after observing that their packaging configurations (types A, B, and C) generated disproportionate disposal volume relative to other SKU categories in the measured aisle range.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Research requires taking multiple perspectives into account, not just operational ones. I did this in three ways. First, the sustainability lens: I connected the slotting problem directly to CEVA’s 95% recycling target. Second, the technology lens: I used regression analysis rather than descriptive statistics — a methodological choice that requires understanding correlation limits and model assumptions. Third, the internationalisation lens: the DESTEP covered EU regulatory context and CEVA’s global logistics footprint, anchoring a warehouse-level study in a company-wide frame.

The scope change is also evidence of critical judgment. When early field data showed inconsistent patterns in replenishment behaviour, I did not average out the noise. I flagged the methodological problem, recommended a correction, and documented the reasoning so it could be challenged. That is what research independence looks like in a live operational environment.

Reflection — Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3, my analytical work was strong on method but I described my research process in terms of what I did, not why I made specific decisions. The PPD3 feedback told me my reflections read like conclusions rather than showing thinking. In Semester 4 I changed that deliberately.

The clearest example is the scope change. In Semester 3 I would have written: “I changed the scope after observing that replenishment data was unreliable.” This semester I can say: I made a judgment call in a live warehouse, presented the reasoning to a supervisor, took ownership of the change, and documented it so it could be challenged. That is a different level of accountability.

The other shift is in tool selection. In Semester 3 I applied frameworks because they were required. In Semester 4 I used regression analysis because it was the right tool for the specific question. That distinction matters for Level 2.

PLO 2

Design

You (re)design a feasible and viable improvement or innovation for a process or product in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence
RSB/RSTB — Zeeman Circular Baby Clothing Project
Three-pillar circular business model for Zeeman (1,300+ stores, 8 European countries). Owned sections 5.1 and 5.2: implementation design and financial feasibility model. Structural error in the model identified and corrected independently.

What I Did

In the RSB/RSTB project my group designed a viable circular business model for Zeeman’s baby clothing line. I owned sections 5.1 and 5.2 — the implementation design and the financial feasibility model. The design has three pillars.

Design for Circularity: switching to mono-material garments certified under GOTS or GRS. I selected mono-material polyester blends over cotton-polyester mixes because GRS-certified recycled polyester maintains a closed loop at end of life. A blended fabric cannot.

Extend the Life: a second-hand resale channel modelled on Het Goed and similar social enterprise sorting operations, where sorting economics work at lower volumes.

Close the Loop: a QR-based Digital Product Passport take-back system, aligned with the EU ESPR regulation that will require DPPs across all textile categories by 2030. The design is built around a regulatory requirement that will eventually be mandatory, not just an innovation for its own sake.

During the project I identified a structural error in the financial model: sorting costs had been applied incorrectly in an earlier version. I took ownership of the correction, rebuilt the Investment tab with corrected logic, and documented what changed and why. The corrected model showed breakeven feasibility for a Netherlands pilot at a realistic return rate, before scaling to all eight European markets.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Design requires justifying design choices, not just describing them. For every major decision in sections 5.1 and 5.2 I wrote an explicit justification. I applied sorting costs to 100% of returned garments rather than a lower assumed rate, because understating processing costs is the most common reason circular economy pilots fail at scale. That is a design decision grounded in real business risk, not a formatting choice.

The financial model correction is also evidence of Level 2 thinking. Catching a structural error mid-project and fixing it independently — rather than accepting the output or delegating it — shows that I treat design as a personal responsibility.

Reflection — Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3, my Decathlon cross-border supply chain design was initially cost-driven. Feedback pushed me to add sustainability and service dimensions — and I added them, but reactively. In Semester 4, the Zeeman design had financial viability, environmental compliance, and regulatory readiness embedded from the start. I built those in because I understood why they mattered, not because feedback told me to.

The other shift is precision. In Semester 3 my design outputs contained approximations I could not fully defend. The Zeeman financial model is built to be challenged: every assumption is visible, every formula references a source cell rather than a hardcoded number. That is the difference between a design that looks complete and one that holds up to scrutiny.

PLO 3

Advise

You advise on possible improvements and/or applicable innovations in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence
CIR3B — Slotting Recommendation, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
Recommendation to relocate 67 cable reel SKUs to bays 023–028, adjacent to waste containers at bay 028. Zero capital expenditure. Scope bounded to the measured aisle range. Supported by regression-derived distance data.

What I Did

After completing the CIR3B research phase, I translated the findings into a concrete recommendation for CEVA warehouse management. The recommendation was specific: relocate 67 cable reel SKU locations to bays 023 to 028, adjacent to existing waste containers at bay 028. Implementation requires only a WMS location update and relabelling — no capital expenditure, no structural change, no dependency on external approval.

I backed every element of the recommendation with data. The regression model showed that trip time is a direct function of distance to the nearest container. I quantified the expected reduction in non-value-adding disposal time by applying the model to the proposed new distances. I also made a deliberate scoping decision: the recommendation covers the identified aisles only, not the full warehouse. Expanding scope beyond the measured area would mean extrapolating beyond the data — an advisory risk I chose not to take.

A second advisory output came from ASCM 4.0, the IMA-FIN business case, where I proposed a data-driven recommendation based on financial analysis. That assignment received a grade of 4.0. I include it here as an honest reflection point rather than as a strong evidence example.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Advise requires substantiating recommendations with data or analyses. The CIR3B recommendation does this at every step: which SKUs, which bays, why zero capex, why limit scope to the measured area — each element is directly traceable to the research output. The decision to bound the scope rather than overgeneralise is itself an advisory judgment. In a professional context, overstating applicability damages credibility. I chose the more conservative framing deliberately.

Reflection — The ASCM 4.0 Result

The ASCM 4.0 result is worth addressing directly. In that assignment I moved too quickly from analysis to recommendation. I had a directional view of what the answer should be and built the recommendation around it rather than letting the data lead. The result was a grade that accurately reflected that gap.

What I learned: a recommendation is only as strong as the data it stands on. In CIR3B I applied that lesson directly. I did not recommend relocating all waste-generating SKUs across the warehouse. I recommended relocating 67 specific SKUs in a defined aisle range, because those are the ones the data supports. That restraint is a direct result of what I got wrong in ASCM 4.0. The learning transferred.

Compared to Semester 3, where my advisory outputs were often well-structured but lacked specific numbers, this semester I can point to a recommendation with a named SKU count, a named bay range, and a model-derived expected outcome. That specificity is the growth.

Connection to Industry Trends

The most direct connection between my academic work and sector practice this semester is the Zeeman DPP project. The EU ESPR regulation will require Digital Product Passports across all textile categories by 2030. I applied this directly by designing a QR-based DPP take-back system for Zeeman, which gave me practical understanding of how circular economy regulation will reshape reverse logistics in European retail. This is not a theoretical exercise — it is a policy that will affect warehouses like the one I work in.

Three trends from this semester connect directly to where logistics is heading. First, circular economy logistics: mandatory take-back systems and DPPs are coming, and any logistics engineer working in retail supply chains will need to understand reverse logistics design. Second, data-driven operations: both the Power BI dashboard and the regression analysis in CIR3B point to the same shift in the profession — analytical output is increasingly expected alongside operational output. Third, warehouse optimisation under labour constraints: the CIR3B methodology is transferable to other SKU categories and other sites.

Three Development Moments This Semester

I can point to three specific moments this semester where my thinking shifted, each traceable to a real decision and a visible consequence.

The first is the scope change in CIR3B. It would have been easier to continue with the original replenishment scope. Instead I flagged the problem early, took responsibility for the change, and documented it. The pattern I am building: when something is not working, name it early and fix it properly.

The second is the proactive AI compliance flag in ASCM. During the Power BI build I identified a potential compliance boundary, flagged it to the course coordinator in writing, and received written clarification before proceeding. That was not required of me. I did it because I understood the implications of getting it wrong, and because I wanted the output to be fully defensible.

The third is the learning transfer from ASCM 4.0 to CIR3B. After receiving a 4.0 for the advisory assignment, I did not treat it as a grading outcome to accept and move past. I used it to recalibrate how I scope recommendations. The direct effect is visible in how the CIR3B output was bounded. That transfer is what professional development actually looks like.

In Semester 3 the Develop PLO was the weakest section of my portfolio. I wrote about industry trends at a surface level and the connection to my own practice was generic. The feedback was that my reflections described what I did without showing what changed.

This semester I can name specific moments where my thinking shifted, each traceable to a real situation and a visible consequence. That is different from reporting activities.
