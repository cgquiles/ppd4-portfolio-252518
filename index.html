PLO 1

Research

You research supply chain challenges in a broader context, taking sustainability, internationalisation, and technology into consideration.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Duo Project
CIR3B: Waste Disposal Time Study, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
I proposed the project and identified the problem. My specific contributions: proposing cable reel SKUs as the research focus, collecting SAP data, co-executing field observations in aisles 316–323, and recommending the scope change from replenishment to picking workers.
Supporting Evidence — Solo
DS&AI A3: Warehouse Zone Optimisation, SEBN Tilburg
Solo analysis of 61,231 outbound delivery lines. I built a five-layer B2B/B2C classifier from scratch because the raw dataset had no pre-existing labels. When Apriori association mining returned zero results, I documented the reason and pivoted to direct co-pick analysis.

What I Did and What Was Mine

CIR3B was a duo project. My classmate and I worked together on the DESTEP analysis, the regression modelling, and the field observations. What I specifically initiated was identifying cable reel SKUs as the focus. I noticed during my own shifts at CEVA that cable reel packaging generates significantly more waste than other SKU categories in that area, and I proposed this to my classmate as the target. That decision shaped the entire study.

The scope change from replenishment workers to picking workers was also my call. Early observations showed replenishment patterns were too variable to produce reliable data. I flagged this to my classmate first, then presented the reasoning to the CIR3A lecturer to get sign-off. I documented the change and we continued on the revised basis.

For DS&AI Assignment 3, everything was solo. The key research judgment was recognising that Apriori was the wrong tool for this dataset. The median B2C basket at SEBN contains only one SKU, so there are almost no multi-item baskets to mine. Rather than lower the thresholds until something appeared, I tested three different settings, documented why all three failed, and switched to direct delivery-level co-pick analysis. That decision produced a more interpretable result than Apriori would have anyway.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Research requires taking sustainability, internationalisation, and technology perspectives into account, not just the operational question. In CIR3B, the sustainability angle was real: CEVA has waste reduction targets, and reducing unnecessary picker travel supports those directly. The technology angle was regression analysis over simple averages. In DS&AI, the internationalisation angle was built into the data: four warehouses across the Netherlands, UK, Italy, and Sweden, with the Netherlands site selected as focus based on its B2C share.

Both projects also show critical judgment about the research process itself. In CIR3B I changed the scope when the data showed the original approach was flawed. In DS&AI I changed the method when Apriori produced nothing useful. Level 2 is not just about applying multiple frameworks; it is about knowing when to adjust.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3, I was good at executing methods but weak at explaining why I chose them or why I changed course. My PPD3 reflections described outcomes rather than decisions. This semester the difference is in the scope change moment. In Semester 3 I would have mentioned it briefly. This semester I can explain the reasoning: the data had too much variance, continuing would have produced an unreliable result, so I stopped, proposed a change, got sign-off, and documented it. That kind of professional accountability was not visible in my Semester 3 work.

Design

You (re)design a feasible and viable improvement or innovation for a process or product in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Group Project
RSB/RSTB: Zeeman Circular Baby Clothing Project
Group project with shared contributions across the design pillars. My specific ownership: the financial model sections, including identifying and correcting a structural error in the sorting cost calculations and independently rebuilding the Investment tab.

What I Did and What Was Mine

The Zeeman project was a group effort. We developed the three-pillar circular business model together: Design for Circularity (mono-material garments with sustainability certification), Extend the Life (second-hand resale channel), and Close the Loop (a take-back system aligned with EU ESPR regulation). I contributed to all three pillars during discussions and planning, but the financial model was my primary responsibility.

During the financial modelling work, I found that sorting costs had been applied incorrectly in an earlier version of the model. I flagged this to the group, corrected the logic, and rebuilt the Investment tab myself with properly structured formulas. This was my decision to fix and my responsibility to get right. The corrected model showed a more realistic picture of the breakeven conditions for a pilot rollout.

The three design pillars themselves were genuinely collaborative. The idea of using a QR-based Digital Product Passport for the take-back system came out of group discussion, and I cannot claim it as solely mine. What I can say is that I understood why it was the right choice: EU ESPR regulation will require DPPs across textile categories by 2030, so designing around that regulatory timeline makes the proposal viable rather than just innovative.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Design requires justifying choices, not just describing them. The financial model correction is the clearest example of this from my individual work. I did not just find an error and report it. I took ownership, fixed it, and rebuilt it in a way that makes every assumption visible and challengeable. That is a different level of design accountability than producing an output and hoping it is correct.

I also applied sorting costs to 100% of returned garments rather than a lower assumed rate. That was a deliberate choice grounded in real business risk: underestimating processing costs is the most common reason circular economy pilots fail at scale.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3 my design work on the Decathlon cross-border case was initially cost-focused. Feedback pushed me to add sustainability and service dimensions. I added them, but because I was told to. In Semester 4, working on Zeeman, I understood why those dimensions belong in supply chain design before anyone asked. That shift from reactive to proactive is the real growth here. The financial model correction also reflects something I lacked in Semester 3: the willingness to go back and fix something rather than accept a flawed output.

PLO 3

Advise

You advise on possible improvements and/or applicable innovations in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Duo Project
CIR3B: Slotting Recommendation, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
My contribution: proposing the slotting recommendation, scoping it to the measured area only, and defending the scope change to the lecturer. The recommendation requires only a WMS location update, no capital expenditure.
Supporting Evidence — Solo
DS&AI A3: Phased Pilot Recommendation, SEBN
Solo advisory output: relocate three specific Zone C SKUs to Zone A as a low-risk pilot. Priority scoring using B2C benefit divided by B2B risk, because all 364 candidates also serve B2B operations and no move is risk-free.

What I Did and What Was Mine

In CIR3B, the recommendation to relocate cable reel SKUs to bays near the waste containers came from my analysis of the data. Bay 028 is where the waste containers are located, and the regression output showed that reducing walking distance directly reduces disposal time. I proposed this to my classmate and we aligned on it together. What was specifically mine was defending the scope change to the lecturer, which was the decision that made the recommendation possible in the first place. Without narrowing to picking workers handling cable reels, the data would not have been clean enough to support a specific recommendation.

The reason no relabelling is needed, only a WMS update, is because the SKUs stay in the same physical area; only their system location changes to trigger replenishment to the new spots. This makes the recommendation executable without any capital expenditure or operational disruption.

For DS&AI, the advisory challenge was that every relocation candidate also appears in B2B same-zone deliveries. There are no risk-free moves. Rather than ignoring this constraint or presenting a clean list, I introduced a priority scoring method: B2C benefit divided by B2B risk plus one. This is parameter-free and produces a ranking that warehouse management can act on with a clear understanding of the trade-off.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Advise requires substantiating recommendations with data or analysis. Both outputs do this. The CIR3B recommendation is traceable from the regression output to a specific area and a specific intervention. The DS&AI recommendation names the constraint explicitly and uses a justified scoring method to work around it. In both cases, the scope of the advice matches the scope of the evidence. That discipline is something I had to learn.

Reflection: The ASCM Pitch Feedback

The ASCM Pitch received an overall grade of 7.1, but the component breakdown is worth being honest about. The Q&A component was marked insufficient because I did not name the most relevant KPI for the network problem. The Insight section was marked poor because my forward-looking reflections lacked depth and specificity. I also ran out of time during the presentation.

That feedback was accurate. A good advisory output is not just the written recommendation; you have to be able to defend it under questioning and connect it to the operational reality. In CIR3B and DS&AI this semester, I focused on making recommendations that I could fully defend: bounded by the data, with every assumption named, and with constraints acknowledged rather than ignored. The difference between those outputs and the ASCM Pitch is visible if you compare them.

Three trends from this semester connect directly to where logistics is heading. Third, omnichannel warehouse complexity: the DS&AI project dealt directly with the tension between B2B and B2C operations sharing the same physical space and the same SKUs. Four Specific Development Moments

The PPD4 oral assessment feedback noted that I should work on turning my interest in analytics into a concrete project with an implementable result. The DS&AI Assignment 3 is exactly that: real data, a real business question, a working dashboard, and a structured recommendation with a pilot plan. The feedback was accurate when it was given. The second moment is the scope change in CIR3B. Rather than continuing with an approach that was producing unreliable data, I named the problem, proposed the change, and got sign-off. That instinct, name problems early and fix them properly, is something I am actively building.

The third is the proactive AI compliance flag in ASCM. During the Power BI build I identified a potential compliance boundary and raised it in writing with the course coordinator before proceeding. That was not required. I did it because I understood what was at stake and wanted the output to be fully defensible.

The fourth is what I learned from the ASCM Pitch feedback. The Q&A component scored insufficient and the Insight section scored poor. Rather than treating that as a grade to accept and move on from, I used it to change how I approach advisory outputs. The effect is visible in CIR3B and DS&AI: bounded scope, named constraints, defensible scoring methods.

In Semester 3 the Develop section of my portfolio was the weakest. I wrote about industry trends without connecting them to my own practice. The feedback said my reflections described what I did without showing what changed. This semester I can point to four specific moments where something shifted.
