Introduction

I am Carlos Granados Quiles, a Year 2 Logistics Engineering student on the MBO track. Alongside my studies I work full-time at CEVA Logistics in Tilburg, which means every academic project this semester had a real operational context behind it. When I designed a waste disposal study for CIR3B, I was working in the same warehouse. When I built the Power BI dashboard for ASCM, I was applying tools I already use professionally. When I analysed outbound delivery data for DS&AI, it was from a site I know well.

This portfolio covers five PLOs. For each one I explain what I did, what was my individual contribution, and what changed in my thinking compared to Semester 3. PLO 1

Research

You research supply chain challenges in a broader context, taking sustainability, internationalisation, and technology into consideration.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Duo Project
CIR3B: Waste Disposal Time Study, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
A time study investigating how much non-value-adding time picking workers spend on waste disposal, and what drives it. My specific contributions: proposing the project, identifying cable reel SKUs as the research focus, collecting SAP data, co-executing field observations, and recommending the scope change from replenishment to picking workers.
Supporting Evidence — Solo
DS&AI A3: Warehouse Zone Optimisation, SEBN Tilburg
Independent analysis of outbound delivery data from a logistics warehouse in the Netherlands. The goal was to identify whether co-locating frequently co-picked SKUs in the same warehouse zone could reduce picker travel time, without disrupting other operations. All analysis, modelling, and the final dashboard were done solo.

What I Did and What Was Mine

CIR3B was a duo project. My classmate and I worked together on the DESTEP analysis, the regression modelling, and the field observations. What I specifically initiated was the focus on cable reel SKUs. During the first visit for the time study, I noticed that cable reel packaging generates significantly more waste than other SKU categories in that area of the warehouse, and I proposed this to my classmate as the research target. That decision shaped the entire study.

The scope change from replenishment workers to picking workers was also my call. Early observations showed replenishment patterns were too variable to produce reliable data. I flagged this to my classmate first, then presented the reasoning to the CIR3A lecturer to get sign-off. I documented the change and we continued on the revised basis.

For DS&AI Assignment 3, everything was solo. The dataset contained 61,231 outbound delivery lines from a warehouse in Tilburg covering the full year 2022, but had no pre-existing B2B or B2C labels. I built a five-layer classifier from scratch to label each delivery, then used association rule mining (Apriori) to look for co-purchase patterns. Apriori works by finding products that are frequently ordered together, which would indicate which SKUs should share a warehouse zone. However, the data showed that most B2C deliveries at this site contain only one SKU, meaning there are almost no multi-item baskets to analyse. I tested three different threshold settings, all returned zero results, and concluded the issue was the data structure itself, not the parameters. I then switched to direct delivery-level analysis, which was more appropriate for this dataset and produced actionable results.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Research requires taking sustainability, internationalisation, and technology perspectives into account alongside the operational question. In CIR3B, the sustainability angle was real: CEVA has waste reduction targets, and reducing unnecessary picker travel supports those directly. The technology angle was the use of regression analysis over simple averages. In DS&AI, the internationalisation angle was built into the data: four warehouses across the Netherlands, UK, Italy, and Sweden, with the Netherlands site selected as focus based on its share of consumer orders.

Both projects also show critical judgment about the research process. In CIR3B I changed the scope when the data showed the original approach was flawed. In DS&AI I changed the method when the primary technique proved unsuitable and documented exactly why. That kind of adjustment, and the transparency around it, is what Level 2 research independence looks like.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

At the start of this semester I would have continued with the original replenishment scope and worked around the data quality problems. By week four of the project I had learned to question whether the measurement approach itself was valid, not just the results it was producing. That shift, from executing a plan to evaluating whether the plan is right, is the clearest growth I can point to in Research.

In Semester 3 my PPD3 reflections described outcomes rather than choices. This semester I can explain the reasoning behind decisions: the data had too much variance, continuing would have produced an unreliable result, so I stopped, proposed a change, got sign-off, and documented it. That kind of professional accountability was not visible in my earlier work.

PLO 2

Design

You (re)design a feasible and viable improvement or innovation for a process or product in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Group Project
RSB/RSTB: Zeeman Circular Baby Clothing Project
A circular economy design project for Zeeman, a European value clothing retailer. The group designed a circular business model covering design, resale, and take-back. My specific ownership: the financial model, including identifying and correcting a structural error in the sorting cost calculations and independently rebuilding the finance tabs.

What I Did and What Was Mine

The Zeeman project was a group effort. The assignment required designing a viable circular business model for Zeeman's baby clothing range. We developed three pillars together: Design for Circularity (switching to mono-material garments with sustainability certification, to enable end-of-life recyclability), Extend the Life (a second-hand resale channel), and Close the Loop (a product take-back system aligned with EU ESPR regulation, which will require Digital Product Passports across textile categories by 2030). The design choices and the direction were genuinely collaborative.

My primary individual responsibility was the financial model. During the modelling work I found that sorting costs, the cost of processing returned garments, had been applied incorrectly in an earlier version. I flagged this to the group, corrected the logic, and rebuilt the finance tabs myself with properly structured formulas. I also applied sorting costs to 100% of returned garments rather than a lower assumed rate. That was a deliberate choice: underestimating processing costs is the most common reason circular economy pilots fail at scale, and I wanted the model to reflect a realistic picture.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Design requires justifying choices, not just describing them. The financial model correction is the clearest example from my individual work. I did not just find an error and report it. I took ownership of fixing it and rebuilt it in a way that makes every assumption visible and challengeable. That is a different level of design accountability than producing an output and accepting it as correct.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

When I noticed the sorting cost figures looked off, my first instinct was not to flag it immediately. I wanted to understand it first. Some numbers did not match what I knew from real-world context, so I looked up actual sorting cost cases online to check whether our assumptions were grounded in reality. When I confirmed the error, fixing it felt like the only option. Handing it back to the group without a solution would have been easier, but the model was my responsibility.

In Semester 3 my design work on the Decathlon cross-border case was initially cost-focused. Feedback pushed me to add sustainability and service dimensions, and I added them because I was told to. In Semester 4 I understood why those dimensions belong in any serious supply chain design before anyone asked. The financial model correction reflects something else I lacked in Semester 3: the instinct to verify rather than assume, and the willingness to go back and fix rather than accept.

PLO 3

Advise

You advise on possible improvements and/or applicable innovations in the supply chain.
Required Level
Level 2 of 3
Primary Evidence — Duo Project
CIR3B: Slotting Recommendation, CEVA Logistics Tilburg
A recommendation to relocate cable reel SKUs to storage bays adjacent to waste containers, reducing unnecessary picker travel. Requires only a WMS location update, no capital expenditure. My contribution: proposing the recommendation and defending the scope change to the lecturer.
Supporting Evidence — Solo
DS&AI A3: Phased Pilot Recommendation, SEBN
A recommendation to relocate three specific SKUs from one warehouse zone to another as a low-risk pilot. The challenge was that all relocation candidates also appear in other types of orders, so no move is entirely risk-free. The recommendation uses a priority scoring formula that makes this trade-off explicit.

What I Did and What Was Mine

In CIR3B, the recommendation to relocate cable reel SKUs to bays near the waste containers came from my analysis of the regression output, which showed that walking distance directly drives disposal time. I proposed this to my classmate and we aligned on it together. What was specifically mine was defending the scope change to the lecturer, which was the decision that made a reliable recommendation possible in the first place. Without narrowing the study to picking workers handling cable reels, the data would not have been clean enough to support a specific recommendation.

The implementation is also deliberately simple: only a WMS location update is needed, with no relabelling required. The items stay in the same physical area; only their system location changes, so replenishment is always directed to the new spots. This makes the recommendation executable without any capital investment or operational disruption.

For DS&AI, the advisory challenge was that every relocation candidate also appears in other order types. There are no risk-free moves. Rather than ignoring this constraint or presenting a clean list, I introduced a priority scoring method: B2C benefit divided by B2B risk plus one. This is a simple, parameter-free formula that produces a ranking warehouse management can act on with a clear understanding of the trade-off involved.

Why This Shows Level 2

Level 2 Advise requires substantiating recommendations with data or analysis. Both outputs do this. The CIR3B recommendation is traceable from the regression output to a specific area and a specific intervention. The DS&AI recommendation names the constraint explicitly and uses a justified scoring method to work around it. In both cases, the scope of the advice matches the scope of the evidence.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3 my advisory outputs were often well structured but lacked specific numbers and clear boundaries. This semester both CIR3B and DS&AI produced recommendations with named constraints, bounded scope, and defensible scoring. The shift came partly from the ASCM Pitch feedback earlier this semester, where the Q&A component was marked insufficient because my answers were not specific enough. That was accurate feedback. It changed how I approach the advisory stage of any project.

Develop

Connection to Industry Trends

Three trends from this semester connect directly to where logistics is heading. First, circular economy regulation: the EU ESPR will require Digital Product Passports across textile categories by 2030. Working on the Zeeman project gave me practical understanding of what that regulatory shift requires in terms of supply chain and product design. Second, data-driven operations: both the Power BI dashboard and the DS&AI zone optimisation project reflect the same shift in the profession. Analytical output is increasingly expected alongside operational output in logistics roles. Third, omnichannel warehouse complexity: the DS&AI project dealt directly with the tension between different order types sharing the same physical space and the same products. This is a live challenge in the warehouse where I work.

Three Development Moments This Semester

The first is the scope change in CIR3B. Rather than continuing with an approach that was producing unreliable data, I named the problem, proposed the fix, got sign-off from the lecturer, and documented the change. That instinct, naming problems early and fixing them properly rather than working around them, is something I am actively building as a professional habit.

The second is what I took from the ASCM Pitch. During the presentation I ran out of time, which affected the quality of the Q&A. The Q&A component was marked insufficient because my answers were not specific enough about the operational context. Looking back, the issue was not just preparation; I had not made the connection between the analysis and the operational reality tight enough. That gap was visible under questioning. It changed how I approach the advisory stage of a project: the recommendation has to hold up when challenged, not just on paper.

The third is the DS&AI project itself. The oral assessment feedback noted that I should work on turning my interest in analytics into a concrete project with an implementable result. By the time of the feedback, I was already working on exactly that: a real dataset, a real business question, a working dashboard, and a structured recommendation. The feedback was accurate when it was given. The semester ended with a direct answer to it.

Reflection: Growth since Semester 3

In Semester 3 the Develop section of my portfolio was the weakest. I wrote about industry trends without connecting them to my own practice. The feedback said my reflections described what I did without showing what changed. This semester I can point to three specific moments where something shifted, each traceable to a real decision and a visible consequence. That is the difference between reporting activities and actually developing as a professional.
